Introduction

Arriving in the US from warm India, I wasn't ready for Wisconsin's cold. As a student, I needed affordable warmth without supporting fast fashion. Upshift, Madison's swap shop, became my go-to. Their focus on sustainability and community inspired me to redesign their website, eager to help their cause thrive




My Role

I have been the UX designer for this project. Starting at my preliminary research, brainstorming and all the design steps. With wonderful guidance from my Professor Adam Rule, I have been able to enhance the User Experience of the website.

Problem

Getting hold of sustainable yet affordable items is not easy in this day and economy. It is a rarity to find affordable yet high quality items online. Upshift Swap Shop is a one stop shop for it. Although, it poses the issue of a low impact online presence.

PROBLEM #1

Sparse Visuals

The website lacks visually appealing elements such as high-quality product images, a vibrant color palette or engaging graphics, detracting from the overall fun and excitement of the thrifting experience.

EXAMPLE

The website looks lackluster with a grey and white palette and overuse of text.

PROBLEM #2

Limited shopping options

The website does not provide an option for users to do much apart from gaining information. The option of viewing or buying items online is impossible due to low interactability of website.

EXAMPLE

You can view information to swap in store but nothing pertaining to being able to see items available at the moment or a way to buy them remotely.

PROBLEM #3

Lacks a pull factor

The features, copywriting and visual elements do not speak to the brand. They lack a pull-factor to attract customers

Brainstorming

After researching upon Upshift's contemporaries and creating a moodboard of visual and potential features for inspiration. I did a 2 hour long thorough brainstorming of ideas to make it more fun while keeping it budget-friendly, without barring the "wild ideas".


After exploring a variety of brainstormed ideas, such as gamification, alerts and gifting hampers , I honed in on three themes that lay closer to the Ideo's Desirability, Feasibility and Viability theory.

  • Gamification of item swapping:To increase customer loyalty by giving points that they can use in store/online or have different badges for each level they reach and a special surprise on achieving it.
  • Alerting customers about new collection: To excite them and keep them in loop with the store in real time hence increasing a sense of personalization.
  • Having gift hampers at low costs: To give customers an occasion to thrift for, they would be able to make customized gift hampers and be able to give high quality yet affordable gifts to their loved ones, online.

Paper Prototype

I conducted my paper prototyping with the students that I tutor, namely, Rachel Ren, Blake and Valentine Schneider. I gave each of them a chance to separately perform each task without looking at others perform it to get independent insights.

Link to the Paper Protypes

The insight are as below -

  • Rachel initially missed the styling tips feature while browsing the website, indicating a need for better visibility and attention-grabbing design.
  • Blake encountered confusion when trying to locate loyalty points, exacerbated by the sidebar label "Sustainability points" instead of "Loyalty Points," highlighting the importance of clear labeling and navigation.
  • Valentine's experience was relatively smooth, but they suggested moving the profile section to a more prominent location for easier access.

Learnings
  • Be open to change, even at later stages
    It took a long while to be able to change my designs after sticking to a couple layout decisions for weeks. As I focused on consistency and making my screen designs look more homely/familiar. I took major shifts in my prototype design towards the end.
  • Visual Design and Branding
    I learnt to explore principles of visual design, including color theory, typography, and imagery selection and to develop a cohesive visual identity that reflects the thrift store's brand values and resonates with its target audience.
  • Having a solid problem statement
    I learned how crucial an effective problem statement is just to create value and not wander off in the process of designing. After 3-4 weeks of drafting my initial problem statement was I able to stike my final one.
